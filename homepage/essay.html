Vanishing

The talk of my elementary school was about a building that’s a 30 minute drive away. A building named Triangle Plaza, home to 3 businesses. A Toys R Us, a Party City, and a Multiplex Cinemas. The building was quite big, allowing each business to have a lot of space. The Multiplex Cinemas had space available to host birthday parties and each auditorium had reclining chairs with trays attached in each row. The Toys R Us was lined with aisle after aisle filled with authentic toys while the Party City had so many options available for costumes. Anyone could be whatever they wanted, create with whatever they wanted, and express whatever they wanted. The entire building was luxurious to my classmates and I, a fun excuse to be with each other and show off our creativity with toys, costumes, and movies.

Although this building may not seem like much, it meant a lot for the latino community in my neighborhood. It was better than cheap knock-off toys that didn’t even look like the thing it was a knock-off of or cheap knock-off costumes that barely fit. It was better than renting a DVD and crowding into 1 couch so that we could all watch it together. The existence of that building, filled with so many opportunities, gave us access to something we would have never experienced otherwise.

What may not seem like much to anyone else could be seen as a goldmine by any New Yorker. New Yorkers could be given anything and they would make art, something beautiful, out of nothing. We took that building, which seems like nothing to anyone that had access to what we didn’t, and made so many precious memories there. New Yorkers could take unwanted furniture and cherish them, decorating their entire room around that one piece.

The New Yorkers where genuine toys, costumes that fit correctly, and movie theaters with reclining chairs were common – they already had it all. This building meant nothing to them, just a waste of space that could be used for something beneficial to them instead of to us: the people that held it in a high esteem.

Toys R Us went out of business, followed by Party City, and then the Multiplex Cinemas. Despite Toys R Us’ and Party City’s extreme popularity, they both went out of business for the same reason: people, with resources, would rather stay at home than to physically search out the items they wish to purchase. We didn’t have enough money to support it enough to stay in business – no one had. Due to what the people with most resources wanted, the people with no resources were left with nothing. The building was sold to Tesla, a car company that only the richest can afford. No one in my community would ever dare to utter the company name – we all knew we would never be able to afford any of the cars in reality, no matter how hard we worked.

Despite the loss of this building as a beginning for creativity, as I grew up, I realized that you can find inspiration anywhere across NYC. NYC is not limited to one building, nor are the places where you can find individuality. As soon as you enter any borough, you are greeted by the diverse culture that is created by the New Yorkers that live here. Even though the creative character that has been established is constantly being pushed out, forced out even, almost every day, many New Yorkers constantly persist because it is in our nature to not let anything stop us from being who we truly are: unique.

The constant inflation, the constant intrusion of people wanting to get a latch onto the uniqueness special to New Yorkers yet will never attain it, not even the constant influence that only the richest seem to hold will stop us – New Yorkers will always find their way back, even if they have to start completely from scratch. There is always something to be hopeful about even in the constant struggle of gentrification. Even as bits and pieces of NYC vanish due to the grasp that the New Yorkers with the most privilege have, NYC will always re-establish itself by the true New Yorkers – the ones that encourage creativity.
